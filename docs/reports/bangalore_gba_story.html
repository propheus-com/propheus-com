Civic safety infrastructure is where Bengaluru has the most room to grow relative to its size. URDPFI's norm is 1 fire station per 200,000 residents — at 12.5 million people, the city has significant headroom to invest. Chapter 04

For the next government: five opportunities, data-backed, ready on day one.

The infrastructure gap is real, independently verified, and concentrated in knowable wards. The next government has something previous governments lacked — a precise, ward-level brief. Not a vague promise to "develop Bangalore." Five interventions. Named wards. Measurable targets.

01
GBA-level fire safety programme
Civic safety is the one dimension where the opportunity is city-wide, not ward-level. 51 new fire stations — the gap between today's 12 and the URDPFI norm — cannot be closed ward by ward. A GBA-wide mandate on day one is the only way to move this at the scale the city's growth demands. Every new station built improves coverage for multiple surrounding wards simultaneously.
Civic & Safety · GBA-mandate
02
North Corporation water mission
Govindapura (17%), Mallasandra (15%), Kodigehalli (14%), Hebbal (10%) — these wards represent the clearest water investment opportunity in the city. Aligning with Jal Jeevan Mission and BWSSB to extend piped connections to the ~400,000 residents in North Corp's outer ring would be transformative. The geographic concentration makes it efficient — the same infrastructure push serves multiple wards along the same corridors.
Water & Sanitation · North Corp
03
One health centre per zero-facility ward
Twelve wards across the city currently have no registered health facilities — J.P. Park, Jeevanahalli, Kamalanagara, L.B. Shastri Nagar, Chowdeshwari Nagar among them. One primary health centre per ward is URDPFI's minimum standard, and it's an investment that any new government could realistically deliver within a single budget cycle. The population sizes involved — between 14,000 and 70,000 per ward — mean each centre would see immediate, high utilisation from day one.
Health Access · Multi-corp
04
Bus-stop blitz for mobility-starved wards
63 wards have the most to gain on mobility. Mangammana Palya in South (53,000 residents, 0 bus stops), Venkateshpuram in North (13,000 residents, 0 bus stops), Hegganahalli in West (52,000 residents, 2 stops). BMTC route extensions anchored by URDPFI's 400m walkability norm represent one of the highest-return investments available — at relatively low infrastructure cost, they connect hundreds of thousands of residents to employment, healthcare, and education across the city.
Mobility · South & West Corp
05
School infrastructure in the largest wards
Dodda Bidarakallu alone has room for 14 new primary schools. Kaveripura, 9 more. Across 41 wards in all five corporations, school infrastructure investment would deliver compounding returns — every child who receives better primary education carries that forward. URDPFI's norm is 1 school per 5,000 residents. The outer-ring wards currently running at 1 per 20,000 or more represent the highest-return opportunity in this dimension.
Education · West & North Corp
Chapter 05

Not census data. Walk distance.

Every number in this report comes from a simple but important question: can a resident actually reach this facility on foot — without a vehicle, without planning a journey around it? Census counts tell you a hospital exists somewhere in a ward. Walk-distance coverage tells you what percentage of that ward's area falls within walking range of it. These are profoundly different — and the second one is the one that matters for planning a city.

How we built this

The Digital Atlas: Five Datasets, One Picture of a City

For decades, urban planning in Bengaluru has been shaped by data that is already out of date by the time it is put to use. The census runs every ten years. The last one was in 2011. But the Bengaluru reflected in those tables - its ward boundaries, population counts, facility records, and infrastructure assumptions - no longer represents the city that exists today.

Since then, Bengaluru has expanded rapidly. It has absorbed new towns, grown by millions across corridors that were never mapped as distinct urban entities, and built entire residential ecosystems without a matching view of civic infrastructure around them.

This is the gap Digital Atlas is designed to close: a living, ward-level picture of what Bengaluru looks like today - for the person trying to reach a hospital, put their child on a school bus, access a park, or rely on emergency services; and for the government, planners, and institutions trying to understand where investment is needed next.

At its core, Digital Atlas is built to become the most comprehensive knowledge representation of every place on earth. It curates and connects real-world signals across location, topography, demographics, weather, mobility, public sentiment, traffic, and more to decode how places actually function.

For this report, our AI Agents analyzed 2,600+ facility points across Bengaluru - including hospitals, clinics, schools, fire stations, police stations, and parks - and mapped the relationships between them to build a deeper, more contextual view of the city's civic infrastructure against India's urban development goals.
